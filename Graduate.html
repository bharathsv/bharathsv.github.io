A Model-Based Approach to Non-Uniform Vowel Normalization [pdf]
Master's Thesis, Dept. of EE, IIT-Kanpur, India
Advisor: Prof. S Umesh

There are wide differences in the formants (F1,F2,F3) of vowels spoken by children, male and female speakers. These are usually attributed to the physiological differences in the vocal-tracts of the speakers. Nordstrom and Lindblom [1] proposed a normalization procedure in which the formants are scaled by a constant scale factor based on the estimate of the speaker's average vocal-tract length in open vowels as determined from measurement of F3. This is usually referred to as uniform scaling. Fant [2] then proposed that the scale factor be made a function of both formant number and vowel category. With this approach, Fant claims to reduce the female-male variance to one-half of that remaining after simple uniform scaling of the type suggested by Nordstrom and Lindblom.

I investigated two methods of non-uniform speaker normalization [3], which unlike Fant's method assume no prior knowledge of the vowel category or formant number and yet achieve normalization performance comparable to that of Fant's. In the first method, the basic idea is to scale each formant by its frequency dependent scale factor (and not based on the formant number or vowel category as Fant's method does). In the second approach, a model for the relationship between formant frequencies of two speakers is formulated and is used for normalization. Corresponding to this model, a mapping from physical frequency to an alternate domain is obtained where the warped spectra are shifted versions of one another for similar enunciations. Interestingly, this mapping which is estimated from vowel data is similar to mel-curve.

The following are the research problems that I have worked on as a part and extension of my master's thesis.


Numerical Computation of Frequency-Warping Function for Exponential Scale Factor Model. [3] [pdf]

 The scale factor by which the subject and reference speakers are scaled is modeled as an exponential form. The piece-wise frequency-warping function is determined which is a "mel-like" one. The frequency-warping functions derived from the all of the above methods are similar to mel-warp functions which shows a relationship between the speech domain and the auditory domain.


Model-Based Approach to Non-Uniform Speaker Normalization. [4,5] [pdf]

A model-based speaker normalization procedure is proposed based on the study of the nature of relationships between formant frequencies of speakers and showed that mel-warping function can be used not only from the psycho-acoustic point of view but also from the point of view of speaker normalization. This work got top rating in its review category at IEEE ICASSP 2004. The journal version of the work is currently under review.


Non-Uniform Speaker Normalization Using Frequency-Dependent Scaling Function. [6] [pdf]

 Fant's non-uniform speaker normalization method provides the best prediction of the subject's scale factor for a particular formant of a particular vowel. Since this method requires the knowledge about formant number and vowel category, it cannot be applied for speech recognition. A frequency-dependent scaling function method is proposed which performs speaker normalization better than Fant's method and can applied on a speech recognizer.


Front-End Signal Processing for Improved Speech Recognition. [7] [pdf]

We have proposed and developed a front-end signal processing scheme using Weighted-Overlap Segment Averaging [8] for improved recognition performance, which performs better than the conventional mel-filter bank, which is the state-of-the art in feature extraction for speech recognition.


Investigation of Non-Uniform Speaker Normalization Methods. [9] [pdf]

Different non-uniform speaker normalization methods [2, 10, 11, 12] have been proposed in literature which do not provide any motivation in choosing a particular non-linear model to perform speaker normalization. We investigated our earlier proposed affine model [4] and frequency-dependent scaling function [6] along with these different non-linear models and showed that our affine model is a better model than others both in terms of recognition accuracy and the motivation behind it.

Algebraic Structures in Digital Signal Processing

This was a project carried out for the course Mathematical Structures of Signals and Systems where various algebraic and geometric structures were studied with their relevance to Digital Signal Processing.


Pitch Excited Linear Predictive Speech Coder

Linear prediction based pitch excited speech coder was implemented and mean opinion score was used as a measure to determine the quality of reconstructed speech. This project was carried out as a part of the course Speech Signal Processing. The prediction coefficients were computed using auto-correlation method by solving Wiener-Hopf equation. The cepstral coefficients were used in the estimation of pitch using a variation of Noll's algorithm [1].

References:

[1] A. M. Noll, "Cepstrum pitch determination," Journal. Acoust. Soc. Amer., vol. 41, pp. 293-309, Feb. 1967.


Analog Voice Privacy System using Time-Frequency Segment Permutation Scrambling

The project was carried out as a part of the course Advanced Topics in Digital Filtering. I implemented the robust Time-Frequency Segment Permutation (TFSP) scrambling scheme, which is used in analog voice privacy systems. The spectrogram and the perceptual hearing (mean opinion scores) were used as measures to determine the amount of privacy involved. A given time-series, here speech signal was segmented into blocks in time-domain. Each of these blocks were segmented in frequency-domain using a 4-channel Quadrature Mirror Filter (QMF) bank. The time-frequency units in time-frequency plane were scrambled using a key, which gives robustness from eavesdropping.


Local K-means Algorithm for Color Image Quantization

The project which was done for the course Image Processing implements and discusses the local K-Means clustering algorithm for color image quantization.
